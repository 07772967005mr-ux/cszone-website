1.3.1d — Network Topologies
Component 1: Computer Systems  ·  ~20 min

What is a Network Topology?

A network topology describes the arrangement of devices and how they are connected. OCR J277 requires knowledge of star and mesh topologies (and basic awareness of bus).

Star Topology

All devices connect to a central switch or hub. All data passes through the central device.

Device — Switch — Device
          |
        Device

Advantages:

  • If one cable or device fails, only that device is affected — others continue working
  • Easy to add new devices (just connect to the switch)
  • Easy to manage and troubleshoot (central point)
  • High performance — each device has its own connection to the switch

Disadvantages:

  • If the central switch fails, the whole network fails
  • Requires more cable than other topologies (each device needs its own cable to the switch)
  • Central switch is expensive
Star is the most common topology for modern LANs — most school and office networks use star topology with a central switch.

Mesh Topology

Every device is connected to every other device (full mesh) or to several others (partial mesh). Multiple paths exist between any two devices.

Advantages:

  • Highly fault tolerant — if one connection fails, data can take an alternative route
  • No single point of failure (unlike star's central switch)
  • High reliability — used in critical systems and the internet's backbone
  • Can handle high traffic as multiple paths are available

Disadvantages:

  • Very expensive — requires many cables and connections
  • Difficult to install and manage
  • Full mesh: n(n-1)/2 connections needed for n devices (e.g. 10 devices = 45 connections)
The internet uses a partial mesh topology — routers have multiple connections so data can route around failures.

Star vs Mesh Comparison

FeatureStarMesh
Central device?Yes (switch/hub)No
Single point of failureYes (the switch)No
Fault toleranceModerate (device failure safe, switch failure = network down)High (alternative paths)
Cabling costModerateVery high (many connections)
Ease of managementEasyDifficult
Used inMost LANs (schools, offices)Internet backbone, military, critical infrastructure

Common Mistakes

  • Saying star has no single point of failure — the central switch IS a single point of failure
  • Saying mesh is used in typical LANs — it's too expensive; star is used instead
  • Confusing the central device in star as a router — it's a switch (though routers can serve this role)
  • Forgetting that the internet backbone uses mesh (partial), not star
