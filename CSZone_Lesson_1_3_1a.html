1.3.1a — LAN & WAN
Component 1: Computer Systems  ·  ~20 min  ·  FREE

What is a Network?

A computer network is two or more computing devices connected together to share resources and communicate. Networks allow devices to share files, printers, internet access, and applications.

LAN — Local Area Network

A LAN connects devices over a small geographical area, typically a single building or site (e.g. a school, home, or office).

LAN characteristics:

  • Covers a small geographical area (one building/site)
  • Owned and controlled by the organisation using it
  • Uses network hardware such as switches, routers, and cables or Wi-Fi
  • High data transfer speeds (typically 100 Mbps–10 Gbps)
  • Relatively cheap to set up and maintain
  • Examples: school network, home network, office network

WAN — Wide Area Network

A WAN connects devices or LANs over a large geographical area — sometimes spanning cities, countries, or continents. WANs use infrastructure owned by third parties (e.g. telephone companies).

WAN characteristics:

  • Covers a large geographical area (city, country, worldwide)
  • Infrastructure owned by telecommunications companies, not the user
  • Lower speeds than a LAN for the same connection (though this varies)
  • More expensive than a LAN
  • Connects multiple LANs together
  • Examples: the internet (the largest WAN), a company with offices in multiple cities connected together
The internet is the largest WAN in the world — it connects billions of devices across the globe.

LAN vs WAN Comparison

FeatureLANWAN
Geographical areaSmall (one building/site)Large (cities/countries)
OwnershipOwned by organisationInfrastructure rented/owned by telecoms
SpeedGenerally high (100Mbps–10Gbps)Generally lower (varies widely)
CostRelatively cheapMore expensive
SecurityEasier to secureHarder to secure (public infrastructure)
ExampleSchool, home, officeThe internet, multinational corporate network

Network Hardware

Key devices:

  • Router — connects a LAN to a WAN (e.g. internet). Directs data packets between networks. Assigns IP addresses via DHCP.
  • Switch — connects devices within a LAN. Forwards data only to the intended device (using MAC addresses). More efficient than a hub.
  • Hub — connects devices in a LAN but broadcasts data to all devices (less efficient, largely replaced by switches).
  • NIC (Network Interface Card) — hardware in each device that enables it to connect to a network. Has a unique MAC address.
  • WAP (Wireless Access Point) — provides Wi-Fi connectivity, allowing wireless devices to connect to the network.
  • Transmission media — the physical means of transmitting data: copper cables (ethernet), fibre optic cables, or wireless (Wi-Fi).

Common Mistakes

  • Saying a WAN is always slower than a LAN — modern fibre WANs can be very fast
  • Saying the internet is a LAN — the internet is a WAN (the world's largest)
  • Confusing a switch and a hub — a switch sends data only to the target device; a hub broadcasts to all
  • Forgetting that WAN infrastructure is owned by telecoms companies, not the user
