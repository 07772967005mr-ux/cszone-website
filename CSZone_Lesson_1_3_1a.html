What is a Network?

A computer network is two or more computers or devices connected together to share resources, data, and services. Networks allow sharing of files, printers, internet connections, and applications.

Networks can be categorised by their geographical size:

LAN — Local Area Network

A LAN covers a small geographical area — typically a single building, office, school, or home.

PropertyDetail
Coverage areaSmall: single building / site / home
OwnershipUsually owned and managed by the organisation using it
Connection mediaEthernet cable (wired) or Wi-Fi (wireless)
SpeedTypically fast — 100 Mbps to 10 Gbps
ExamplesSchool network, office network, home Wi-Fi

In a LAN, devices are connected using Ethernet cables or wireless access points (WAPs). The infrastructure (cables, switches, routers) is usually owned by the organisation.

WAN — Wide Area Network

A WAN covers a large geographical area — spanning cities, countries, or even the entire world. The Internet is the largest WAN in existence.

PropertyDetail
Coverage areaLarge: cities, countries, worldwide
OwnershipInfrastructure owned by telecom companies (BT, Virgin, etc.)
Connection mediaTelephone lines, fibre optic cables, satellite links, microwave
SpeedTypically slower than LAN; varies greatly
ExamplesThe Internet, a company's network across multiple offices worldwide

Organisations use leased lines (dedicated connections rented from telecoms companies) or public internet connections to link their LANs across a WAN.

PAN — Personal Area Network

A PAN is a very small network — typically within a few metres of a person. It connects personal devices together.

PropertyDetail
Coverage areaVery small: a few metres
TechnologyBluetooth, NFC, USB, infrared
ExamplesSmartphone to wireless headphones, smartwatch to phone, laptop to Bluetooth keyboard

Comparing LAN, WAN and PAN

FeaturePANLANWAN
Coverage~metresBuilding/siteCities/countries/worldwide
SpeedLow–mediumHighVariable (often lower)
Infrastructure ownerIndividualOrganisationTelecoms company
TechnologyBluetooth, NFCEthernet, Wi-FiFibre, leased lines, satellite
ExampleBluetooth headphonesSchool networkThe Internet

Advantages of Networking

  • Shared resources: printers, scanners, storage can be shared by many users
  • Shared internet connection: one broadband connection shared across many devices
  • Centralised data storage: files stored on a server accessible from any device
  • Communication: email, messaging, video calls
  • Centralised security & updates: easier to apply updates and manage security

Disadvantages of Networking

  • Security risks: networks are a target for hackers and malware
  • Cost of setup: cables, switches, routers, server hardware
  • Dependence on network: if network fails, users cannot access shared resources
  • Data can be intercepted: sensitive data at risk if not encrypted
Exam tip: Know the three types — LAN, WAN, PAN — and their key features: coverage area, typical technology, ownership. The Internet is a WAN. A school network is a LAN. Bluetooth headphones = PAN. OCR questions often ask you to identify network type from a scenario and justify your answer — make sure you explain WHY (e.g. "it covers a small area — one building — so it is a LAN").
⚠️ Common Mistakes
  • Saying "WAN is faster than LAN" — LANs are typically faster; WANs are larger but often slower
  • Saying "the internet is a LAN" — the internet is the world's largest WAN
  • Confusing PAN with LAN — PANs are personal (few metres, Bluetooth); LANs are building-scale
  • Saying organisations own WAN infrastructure — telecom companies do
  • Not justifying answers — "it is a LAN because it covers one school building" scores more marks than just "LAN"

Q5State two differences between a LAN and a WAN.[4]
✅ Mark scheme
LAN covers a small area (building); WAN covers a large area (cities/countries) [1+1]; LAN infrastructure is owned by the organisation; WAN infrastructure is owned by telecoms companies [1+1]. (Or: LAN typically faster than WAN [1+1] with explanation.)
Q6State two advantages of connecting computers in a network.[2]
✅ Mark scheme
Any two: shared resources (printers, storage) [1]; shared internet connection [1]; centralised data storage [1]; communication via email/messaging [1]; centralised software updates/security management [1].
Q7State two disadvantages of networking.[2]
✅ Mark scheme
Any two: security risks — hacking/malware [1]; cost of hardware/setup [1]; reliance on the network — if it fails, shared resources unavailable [1]; data can be intercepted [1].
Q8A hospital connects computers in its reception, wards and operating rooms — all within one large building. A separate branch 200 miles away connects via the Internet. Identify the type of network used (a) within the building and (b) between the two sites. Justify each answer.[4]
✅ Mark scheme
(a) LAN [1] — covers a single building/site [1]; (b) WAN [1] — connects over a large geographical distance (200 miles); uses Internet infrastructure owned by telecoms companies [1].
